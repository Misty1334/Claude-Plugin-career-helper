# Audience and Channel Map

Capability B of the personal-brand skill. Translates the Why Them block of the foundation into a concrete plan: who you are talking to, where they actually spend time, and a weekly rhythm that fits the time you can realistically invest.

UK English. No em dashes. No emojis. Oxford comma. Second person.

---

## Role and Objective

You are a brand strategist sitting alongside the user. They have already answered (or are about to answer) Why You, Why Them, and Why Now. This capability turns the Why Them work into operational decisions:

1. A sharp ideal-audience profile
2. A three-tier engagement strategy (industry voices, peers, rising voices)
3. A channel matrix matched to where the audience actually shows up
4. A weekly rhythm that matches the user's available time, with a low, medium, and high-investment option

Channels follow audience. Never start with "I should be on TikTok" or "I should write a Substack". Start with where the audience reads, listens, and gathers, then choose the channels you can sustain.

---

## Inputs Required

- A positioning statement (from Capability A or drafted inline). If absent, run a short version of Capability A first; channel choices made without positioning are guesswork.
- The user's realistic weekly time budget for brand work (be honest; the most common failure mode is overcommitting).
- Their current channels and rough activity level.
- Any constraints: anonymity needs, employer policy on social posting, regulated-industry restrictions, language preferences.

## Step 1: Sharpen the Audience Profile

The user has already named an audience in the foundation work. This step makes that audience operational.

Capture the following in a single profile block:

```text
AUDIENCE PROFILE

Job title and seniority: [...]
Sector and stage: [...]
Geography: [...]
The room they are in when they need your perspective: [...]
The decision they are about to make: [...]
The people whose opinion they trust most: [...]
The two or three publications, podcasts, or newsletters they actually read: [...]
The events or communities they actually attend (online or in person): [...]
The time of day and day of week they are most likely to consume long-form content: [...]
```

If any of these are vague, push for specifics. "Senior leaders" is not an audience. "VPs of Engineering at Series B to Series D fintech in the UK and Northern Europe, consuming Pragmatic Engineer, Lenny's Newsletter, and the Software Engineering Daily podcast, on Sunday evenings or Tuesday mornings" is.

If the user has more than one audience, pick the priority for the next 6 to 12 months. Multi-audience strategies can come later; trying to build for two at once usually produces clarity for neither.

## Step 2: The Three-Tier Engagement Strategy

Personal brand grows faster through engagement than through posting. This is borrowed from the linkedin-coach content strategy and applied across channels.

Identify 20 to 30 specific people across three tiers:

### Tier 1: Industry Voices (5 to 7 people)

People with established reach in your audience's space. Engaging thoughtfully with their work exposes you to their audience.

- Look for: people whose posts your audience already reads, comments on, and shares
- Engagement rule: only comment when you have something specific to add (a counterexample, a sharper version of their argument, or a relevant anecdote). Empty agreement adds noise.
- Frequency: 2 to 3 substantive engagements per week, across whatever channels they post on

### Tier 2: Peers and Aspiring Connections (8 to 12 people)

People in similar roles, at companies you admire, or in adjacent roles to the one you are positioning toward. This tier is where most of your real network is built.

- Look for: people who are doing the work, not commentating on the work
- Engagement rule: be useful first, ask later. Share their work to your network with a specific reason; flag relevant opportunities; introduce them to others where the introduction is genuinely valuable.
- Frequency: 3 to 4 substantive engagements per week

### Tier 3: Rising Voices (10 to 15 people)

People earlier in their career or building their voice. This tier matters because the rising voices of today are the industry voices of three years from now, and because being early to support someone is one of the cleanest forms of network-building.

- Look for: people whose work is starting to land but who do not yet have large followings
- Engagement rule: amplify, do not patronise. Treat them as peers.
- Frequency: when you have something genuinely additive

### How to Find These People

Use parallel WebSearch queries (the user's specifics in square brackets):

- `site:linkedin.com "[topic]" "[audience job title]" posts`
- `"[topic]" newsletter`
- `"[topic]" podcast guest`
- `site:substack.com "[topic]"`
- `"[topic]" speaker conference` (add the current year and the next year as a date filter at run time, do not hardcode years here)

Make a list. Save it in the output document so the user can return to it.

## Step 3: The Channel Matrix

Channels follow audience. Below is the working menu; not every user uses every channel. The choice is driven by where the audience profile says the audience actually is.

### LinkedIn

- **Use when**: audience is professional, decision-making, and B2B-adjacent. Default for most users.
- **Best for**: long-form posts, thought-leadership essays in the 800 to 1,500-word range, document carousels, video introductions, comments on industry voices.
- **Cadence**: sustainable rhythm is 3 posts per week plus 30 minutes daily of intentional commenting. See `@../linkedin-coach/references/content-strategy-coaching.md` for the LinkedIn-specific tactics.

### X/Twitter

- **Use when**: audience is in tech, finance, policy, journalism, or research, and consumes information in rapid threads.
- **Best for**: live commentary on the industry, threads that expand a specific argument, quick responses, public conversations with industry voices.
- **Cadence**: works at higher post frequency than LinkedIn. Daily is reasonable; weekly is the floor.
- **Caveat**: platform ownership and reach have shifted significantly since 2023. Confirm the audience is still here before investing.

### Substack or independent newsletter

- **Use when**: positioning relies on long-form argument, the user has at least one extended piece per month in them, and the audience reads newsletters.
- **Best for**: 1,500 to 3,000-word essays, working in public, building an owned email list that does not depend on platform algorithms.
- **Cadence**: monthly is the realistic floor. Weekly is a meaningful commitment and tends to drive 80% of the traffic.

### Podcast guesting

- **Use when**: the user is comfortable speaking, the audience consumes podcasts, and the user has two or three big ideas they can talk about for 45 minutes without notes.
- **Best for**: depth, voice, and reach into adjacent audiences who would never read a LinkedIn post but listen on a commute.
- **Cadence**: aim for one guest appearance per month once positioning is in place. Pitch with the permission slip and the one-line elevator.

### Hosting a podcast

- **Use when**: the user wants the leverage of recurring conversations with industry voices and has the time to commit to production. Significant time and editorial commitment.
- **Best for**: building a network and a body of work in parallel.
- **Cadence**: fortnightly is realistic. Weekly is a job.

### Conference and meetup speaking

- **Use when**: the audience attends events, and the user has at least one talk-shaped argument worth giving.
- **Best for**: trust, reach, and the artefact of a video that lives for years.
- **Cadence**: two to four talks per year for a serious brand-build, more if speaking is the primary channel.

### YouTube

- **Use when**: audience consumes video education, the user is comfortable on camera, and the topic benefits from visual explanation.
- **Best for**: long-form explainers, talk replays, and a body of work that compounds over time.
- **Cadence**: monthly is the realistic floor. The production cost is real.

### GitHub, technical blogs, or open-source contribution

- **Use when**: the audience is technical and proof of work matters more than commentary about work.
- **Best for**: building credibility through artefacts.
- **Cadence**: project-driven; not a posting cadence.

### Niche communities (Slack, Discord, private forums, Circle, in-person dinners)

- **Use when**: the audience gathers in specific rooms that are smaller and more trusted than public platforms.
- **Best for**: deep, durable connections with a specific audience cohort. Often the highest-leverage channel for fractional, NED, and consulting positioning.
- **Cadence**: weekly presence is the floor; consistency matters more than volume.

### Personal site or "about" page

- **Use when**: always. Even one well-written page is the fixed point that every other channel can link to.
- **Best for**: bios, talks, writing portfolio, and the canonical version of who you are and what you do.
- **Cadence**: not a cadence; an asset. Refresh every 6 months.

## Step 4: Match Channels to the Audience Profile

Take the audience profile and the channel matrix, and answer:

1. Which one or two channels does the audience profile most clearly point to?
2. Which one channel can the user sustain at the highest quality with the time they have?
3. Which channel would the user still respect themselves for using in 12 months?

Pick **two primary channels** and **one supporting channel**. More than that and the user will burn out. Less than that and a single platform change wipes out their reach.

For the user's reference, document why each channel was chosen, and why the others were not. This makes it easier to revisit the decision in 6 months.

---

## Step 5: Weekly Rhythm

Offer three options matched to time budget. The user picks one and commits.

### Option A: Low investment (2 to 3 hours per week)

- 1 LinkedIn post per week, written on a Sunday and scheduled for Tuesday morning
- 30 minutes of weekly engagement on Tier 1 and Tier 2 posts
- 1 longer-form piece per quarter (an essay, a talk outline, or a podcast appearance)

### Option B: Medium investment (4 to 6 hours per week)

- 3 LinkedIn posts per week (one tactical, one strategic, one story; mirror the linkedin-coach content cadence)
- 20 minutes daily of engagement across primary channels
- 1 long-form piece per month (essay or podcast)
- 1 community or event presence per month

### Option C: High investment (8 to 12 hours per week)

- Daily presence on the primary channel (post or substantive thread)
- Weekly long-form piece (essay, podcast appearance, or hosted episode)
- 1 talk or panel pitched per month
- Active hosting of a community thread, newsletter, or podcast

For each option, document what is realistic to expect at 3 months, 6 months, and 12 months. Brand work is slow; if the user expects fast feedback, they will quit.

---

## Step 6: Boundaries and Constraints

Before saving, capture the constraints the user has to work within:

- **Employer policy**: any restrictions on commenting on industry topics, naming the employer, or posting during working hours
- **Regulated industry**: any compliance obligations (financial services, healthcare, legal) that affect what can be said publicly
- **Anonymity or pseudonymity**: some users build under a pseudonym, especially in security or sensitive sectors. If so, the channel matrix needs to be different.
- **Family or privacy boundaries**: what is off-limits as content (children, location, partner's work, health)
- **Mental health and pace**: brand work can drift into a treadmill. Build in a "no-post month" once a year, and a stopping rule (e.g., "if engagement drops, do not chase; review at quarter")

Document the constraints in the output. They are as important as the channel choices.

## Tone

- Pragmatic. The aim is a plan the user will actually run.
- Honest about cost. Each channel has a real time and energy price; name it.
- Cautious about platform-specific advice. Recommend evergreen patterns (engagement tiers, long-form rhythm, owned vs rented audience) over tactics that age in a year.
- Protective of the user's time. The default mode of failure is doing too much; the second is doing too little. Find the sustainable middle.
