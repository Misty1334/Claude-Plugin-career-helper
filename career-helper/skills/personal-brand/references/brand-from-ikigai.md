# From Ikigai to Brand

A short bridge document. If the user has answered the four ikigai questions in Tim, the answers feed directly into the Why You, Why Them, and Why Now framework. This file shows how, so the brand foundation work does not feel like starting from scratch.

UK English. No em dashes. No emojis. Oxford comma. Second person.

---

## When This File Loads

Load alongside `@personal-brand-foundation.md` when:

- Tim has just walked the user through the four ikigai questions and a clear topic, audience, or sector has emerged
- The user volunteers ikigai-style answers ("I love working with founders, I am good at unpicking technical debt, I care about teams not burning out, and I am paid to advise on architecture")
- A previous session captured ikigai answers in `career-helper-preferences.md`

If none of the above apply, ignore this file and run the foundation work fresh.

## The Mapping

The four ikigai questions in `@../tim/references/tim-ikigai-guide.md` are:

1. What do you enjoy?
2. What are you good at?
3. What does the world need?
4. What can you be paid for?

The brand framework asks three questions:

- Why You
- Why Them
- Why Now

Here is how the answers move across.

### What you enjoy + What you are good at: Why You

The overlap of "enjoy" and "good at" is the user's natural angle. It is what they reach for, that other people also recognise as strength. This is the rawest form of the **distinct angle** in Why You.

What to do with it:

- Use the user's "good at" answers as the first draft of proof points. Push for specifics: "good at unpicking technical debt" is a starting point; "good at unpicking technical debt in 5 to 10-year-old fintech monoliths under regulatory pressure" is a Why You answer.
- Use the user's "enjoy" answers to test voice fit. The pillar that the user enjoys talking about is the pillar they will sustain. The pillar they tolerate is the pillar that will rot.

### What the world needs: Why Them

"What the world needs" is, in this framework, the audience-shaped version of the question: which people in the world have a problem the user cares about?

What to do with it:

- Translate "the world needs" into "this specific audience needs". Push from sector-level statements ("the world needs better AI governance") to person-level statements ("NEDs and trustees inheriting AI systems they did not commission need a way to challenge them").
- Use the user's emotional energy on this question as a signal. The audience the user gets visibly fired up about is the audience they will write for, talk to, and turn up for.

### What you can be paid for: Why Now

"What can you be paid for" is the practical anchor in ikigai. In the brand framework, it overlaps closely with **Why Now**: what is the audience willing to pay attention to, hire for, or fund right now, that connects to the user's distinct angle?

What to do with it:

- Use the "can be paid for" answer to surface the market timing. If the user can be paid for X today, what changed in the last two years that made X pay? That change is the Why Now anchor.
- If there is a gap (the user's "good at" and "the world needs" answers point one way, but the "can be paid" answer points elsewhere), name the gap. Two routes exist: build the proof needed to close the gap (a 6 to 12-month plan), or pick adjacent positioning that monetises today.

## Worked Walk-Through

A user has just answered, in Tim:

- Enjoy: "Working with founders, particularly the awkward conversations no one else wants to have."
- Good at: "Unpicking technical debt and explaining it to non-technical boards."
- World needs: "Boards that understand what they are taking on when they inherit a tech estate they did not build."
- Can be paid for: "Fractional CTO and board advisor work, mostly Series B to Series C."

The bridge into the brand foundation might look like this:

**Why You (first draft, to be sharpened in the foundation work):**

- Distinct angle: "I sit between founders and boards, on the awkward conversations about inherited technical debt." (push for proof points, story arc, three words)
- Proof: fractional CTO work, board-advisor work; specific projects to be confirmed
- Story arc: ask the user to walk through the chapters

**Why Them (first draft):**

- Audience: "Boards inheriting technical estates they did not build, particularly at Series B to Series C." (push for the specific person, the room, the 2am problem)
- Native language: capture from the user
- Consensus view to push against: capture from the user

**Why Now (first draft):**

- Personal: fractional pivot, expertise compounding across multiple boards
- Market: ask the user what has shifted in the last 12 to 18 months that made boards take this seriously

The bridge does not replace the foundation work. It accelerates it. The user does not have to start from a blank page; they start with the four answers as raw material.

---

## What This Bridge Does Not Do

- It does not commit the user to the position. The ikigai answers are provisional. Some of them get rejected during the foundation work, and that is fine.
- It does not invent proof. Just because the user "enjoys awkward founder conversations" does not mean the user has done them at scale. Confirm before drafting.
- It does not skip Why Now. "Can be paid for" is necessary but not sufficient. The market timing question still needs to be answered.

---

## When to Skip the Bridge

- The user has not been through the ikigai questions and has clear positioning already. Run the foundation work directly.
- The user has been through the ikigai questions but found the exercise unhelpful (`direction_questions_declined: true` in preferences). Do not lean on those answers; run the foundation work fresh.
- The ikigai answers are very thin or were given reluctantly. The bridge will produce thin Why You answers. Run the foundation work fresh and use the ikigai answers only as a check at the end.
