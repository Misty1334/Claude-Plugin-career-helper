# Fractional and Portfolio Personal Brand Guide

Persona reference for the personal-brand skill. Loads alongside the standard capability references when the user is positioning for fractional, portfolio, or independent consulting work.

UK English. No em dashes. No emojis. Oxford comma. Second person.

---

## When to Load This Guide

Trigger signals from the user:

- "I am going fractional" or "I am building a portfolio"
- "I want inbound for fractional CTO, CMO, COO, CPO, or CFO work"
- "I have left my full-time role and am setting up as an advisor or consultant"
- "I want to attract Series A to Series C clients"
- "I want my brand to do the heavy lifting so I am not always pitching"

If the user has not yet decided whether to go fractional, route to `/career-transitions` first for the structural and financial decision; return to this skill for the brand layer once they have committed.

## Why Fractional Brand Is Different

Fractional positioning has constraints that employed brand work does not:

- **The brand is the funnel.** Employed leaders can be quiet and still get promoted. Fractional leaders cannot. Inbound is the cheapest pipeline; brand is what creates it.
- **Buyers are sceptical of "fractional" by default.** Many founders have hired the wrong fractional and got burned. The brand has to overcome that scepticism in seconds.
- **The decision is made in five minutes or less.** Founders triage potential fractional hires fast. The first sentence of any bio, post, or About page must do work.
- **Pricing is positioning.** A vague brand attracts low-budget enquiries. A sharp brand attracts the right kind of buyer at the right kind of price.
- **Word-of-mouth is the long game.** Most fractional contracts come from an existing client referring the user to another founder. The brand has to be referrable: easy to describe in one line, easy to introduce by email, easy to remember.

These constraints shape every block of the framework.

## Why You: Adjustments for Fractional Brand

The distinct angle for a fractional should be one of:

- **Stage specialism**: "I am the CTO Series A founders bring in once they have shipped product-market fit and are about to over-hire", "I am the CFO for the moment a company crosses 25 employees and needs proper finance"
- **Sector specialism**: "fractional CMO for B2B vertical SaaS in regulated sectors", "fractional COO for marketplaces between Series A and B"
- **Problem specialism**: "fractional CTO for companies inheriting a tech estate after acquisition", "fractional CFO for first-time founder fundraising"
- **Operator-led specialism**: "operator, not advisor, two prior exits"; the implicit contrast is with consultants who have not done the job
- **Combination**: usually two of the above, never four

Adjustments to the foundation work:

- **Proof points must include the buyer's risk reducers.** Buyers want to know: have you done the role at this stage, in this sector, with this kind of founder? Capture proof against each risk-reducer, with specific contracts, results, or scars.
- **Story arc must explain the move to fractional.** The most credible answer is a combination of "I have done this enough times to recognise the pattern, and I want to help more companies through it without staying full-time at one".
- **Three words must include a positioning claim.** Not "experienced" or "passionate". Words like "operator", "stage-specific", "regulated", "B2B SaaS", "post-PMF", "second-time founder". Specific words that signal fit.
- **The permission slip is your single highest-leverage paragraph.** It is what gets pasted into a founder's introduction email. Spend more time on this than on anything else.

## Why Them: Adjustments for Fractional Brand

The audience is not "founders". It is:

- **Specific stage**: pre-seed, seed, Series A, Series B, Series C, growth, post-acquisition, scaleup
- **Specific sector**: B2B SaaS, fintech, climate-tech, marketplaces, deeptech, healthtech, consumer
- **Specific role-buying moment**: first product hire, first engineering manager, first finance hire, post-PMF over-hiring, post-fundraise team scaling, pre-Series-B operational rigour
- **Specific company size or ARR band**: <£1m ARR, £1m to £5m, £5m to £20m
- **Specific founder profile**: first-time, second-time, technical, non-technical, sector-native, sector-outsider

The 2am problem for a founder considering a fractional is not "we need another senior person". It is "I am about to hire the wrong person full-time and I cannot afford the mistake; I need someone who can tell me whether to hire and who". The brand needs to position the user as the answer to that specific question, not as a generic senior leader.

Native language to capture from buyers:

- "post-PMF", "scaling team", "first finance hire", "fractional", "interim", "advisor", "operator", "exec coach"
- Stage and sector shorthand the audience uses with each other ("pre-revenue", "pre-Series B", "post-product-market fit")
- The honest internal language ("I do not know what good looks like", "I have hired the wrong person twice", "we are flying blind on X")

## Why Now: Adjustments for Fractional Brand

The "why now" answer for fractional is a combination of:

- **Macro funding climate**: which stage of company is currently struggling to hire full-time at senior levels and turning to fractional instead
- **Market shifts in the audience's sector**: AI, regulation, channel shift, platform shift, downturn-driven discipline, new buyer behaviour
- **Personal arc**: the user's own readiness to do this kind of work credibly (number of companies, number of cycles, range of contexts)

The strongest "why now" is "the market needs this stage of help right now, and I am one of the few people who has done it enough times to do it cleanly".

Cost of inaction for the buyer: a specific, costly mistake the buyer is likely to make in the next 6 months without the user's help. Name it. It is what justifies a day rate.

## Channel Adjustments for Fractional Brand

Fractional brand is built on:

- **LinkedIn**: the dominant channel for B2B fractional. Daily presence is realistic and rewarded. See `@../linkedin-coach/references/fractional-linkedin-guide.md` for tactics.
- **Personal site or "work with me" page**: the buyer's destination after they read a post or get a referral. Treat this as the highest-leverage page on the internet for fractional work.
- **Targeted inbound channels**: founder Slacks, sector-specific communities, accelerator networks, alumni networks. Often higher conversion than broad social.
- **Podcast guesting**: well-targeted founder-audience podcasts. One good appearance is worth ten medium-quality LinkedIn weeks.
- **Substack or independent writing**: if the user has long-form in them, this channel compounds. Founders share substantive essays in DMs in a way they do not share LinkedIn posts.
- **Referrals and endorsements**: the lowest-cost, highest-conversion pipeline. Build the brand to be referrable; ask happy clients for specific written endorsements at clear moments.

Channel matrix weighting:

- Two primary channels (almost always LinkedIn plus the personal site)
- One supporting channel (community presence, podcast guesting, or long-form)
- Inbound infrastructure (a working contact form, a clear "what I do, who for, how to start" page, pricing signals)

## Bio Library Adjustments

Add to the standard bio library:

- **"Work with me" page** (treat as a bio surface). One paragraph for what you do, one paragraph for who you are right for, one paragraph for who you are not right for, one paragraph for how to start. This page is the conversion page; spend disproportionate time on it.
- **First-message-to-founder template** (a private bio surface). The 50- to 80-word reply you send when a founder DMs "do you do fractional CTO work?". Treat this as a bio you draft once and reuse.
- **Pricing-signal line in LinkedIn About**: if the user is comfortable with it, a single sentence indicating range or commitment level (e.g., "Working with two to three founders at a time on three- to six-month engagements"). Filters enquiries up front.

The bio library should explicitly include the audience and the role-buying moment. Generic fractional bios attract generic enquiries; specific bios attract specific work at better terms.

## Cadence Adjustments

Fractional brand benefits from higher cadence than employed brand. The medium-investment option in the cadence template (3 LinkedIn posts per week, daily commenting, monthly long-form, monthly podcast or talk) is the default starting point, not an upper bound.

Caveats:

- **Do not post more than the user can sustain.** A six-month-empty profile reads worse than a profile that posts twice a month consistently.
- **Build a pipeline of evergreen posts** before going public. Five drafts banked is a stress-reducer.
- **Engagement matters as much as posting.** Founders DM after seeing a thoughtful comment on someone else's post; they DM less after seeing a polished post in isolation.

## Constraints and Risks Specific to Fractional Brand

- **Confidentiality**: client work is usually confidential. The brand has to demonstrate competence without quoting clients. Use anonymised case studies (with client permission) or "patterns I have seen across X engagements" framing.
- **Conflict of interest**: do not post or comment on competitors of current clients. Document a conflict-check rule in the constraints section of the audience-channel-map.
- **IR35 and contractor status (UK)**: positioning that reads as employment can have tax implications. Bios should clearly signal independent fractional work.
- **Pipeline cliff**: most fractional careers have a quiet quarter once a year. Build the brand to compound through quiet quarters, not just spike during busy ones. Long-form essays and podcast episodes that live for years are the antidote.

## Tone

- Confident, not performative. Buyers can smell performance.
- Specific over general. Every adjective should be replaced with a fact.
- Honest about cost. Fractional brand is a 12-month build for most users, with a short tail of luck. Set expectations.
- Protective of voice. Many fractional bios start to sound the same; the user's voice is the moat.
